# Decision Criteria for the Comparative Legal Analysis

## Scope and analytical approach

The comparative legal analysis assessed de jure tax harmonization across 48 tax features within each Regional Economic Community (REC). The analysis covers:

* 22 corporate income tax features;
* 13 personal income tax features;
* 5 value-added tax features;
* 1 excise-duty feature, structured through 28 product groups; and
* 7 property and wealth tax features.

Perfect uniformity across all member states was not assumed. Instead, countries within each REC were grouped according to the similarity of their legal rules or tax features. These feature-specific groups formed the basis for the subsequent HHI-based assessment of harmonization.

The classification of the 48 features into qualitative and structural, quantitative, binomial, and mixed variables is set out in `data_types.md`.

## General rules

### Qualitative and structural variables

Qualitative and structural variables were assessed according to the similarity of legal classifications, functional design, or regulatory structure. Countries were treated as harmonized where they displayed substantially similar legal patterns for the relevant feature.

### Quantitative variables

Quantitative variables were assessed through feature-specific bands. For tax-rate variables, countries were treated as harmonized where the difference between the highest and lowest rate within a group did not exceed five percentage points. The procedure was repeated for the remaining observations to identify further groups and outliers.

The five-percentage-point threshold refers to an absolute difference in percentage points. Thus, rates of 20% and 25% fall within the same band, whereas rates of 20% and 26% do not.

### Binomial variables

Binomial variables were classified according to the presence or absence of the relevant legal rule, tax, or regime. Countries with the same outcome were treated as belonging to the same harmonized group.

## 1. Corporate Income Tax

### 1.1. Tax Liability

#### Corporate tax residence

Corporate tax residence was classified according to the connecting factors used to establish residence. Two principal patterns were identified:

* **Formation-based criteria**, including incorporation, registered office, and legal seat; and
* **Substance-based criteria**, including place of effective management, principal place of business, principal place of management, and management seat.

Countries were treated as harmonized where their residence rules reflected similar formation-based, substance-based, or combined patterns.

#### Corporate income tax base

Corporate income tax bases were classified according to whether taxation followed a territorial, worldwide, or mixed approach. The assessment distinguished the treatment of active and passive income. Countries were treated as harmonized where they followed similar approaches for both categories of income.

#### Group treatment

The availability of group treatment was assessed as a binomial variable. Countries were grouped according to the presence or absence of a group-treatment regime.

### 1.2. Taxable Income

#### Loss carry-forward

Loss carry-forward was assessed in two stages.

First, countries were distinguished according to the existence or absence of a loss carry-forward provision.

Second, where a finite carry-forward period applied, countries were grouped within a two-year band. This threshold was derived from the cross-country standard deviation of finite carry-forward periods (SD=1.46).

Indefinite carry-forward provisions were excluded from the standard-deviation calculation and treated as a separate harmonized group.

#### Corporate tax incentives and preferential regimes

Corporate tax incentives and preferential regimes were classified through 13 incentive categories:

| No. | Incentive category                                                  |
| --: | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
|   1 | Mining and natural resources                                        |
|   2 | Agriculture, fisheries, and food                                    |
|   3 | Industry and manufacturing                                          |
|   4 | Energy and environment                                              |
|   5 | Tourism and hospitality                                             |
|   6 | Financial and management services                                   |
|   7 | Transport and logistics                                             |
|   8 | Technology, research and development, and digitalisation            |
|   9 | Regional incentives and free zones                                  |
|  10 | Export                                                              |
|  11 | Small and medium-sized enterprises, entrepreneurship, and start-ups |
|  12 | Employment and education                                            |
|  13 | General investment and tax regimes                                  |

Countries were treated as harmonized where their assigned incentive categories displayed at least 60% overlap.

#### Participation relief

Participation relief was assessed according to the existence and legal direction of relief for dividend income. Countries were grouped where their treatment of relevant inbound and outbound dividend income followed similar patterns.

#### Unilateral double-taxation relief

The availability of unilateral double-taxation relief was assessed as a binomial variable. Countries were grouped according to the presence or absence of such relief.

### 1.3. Tax Rates

#### Corporate income tax rate

The general corporate income tax rate applicable to resident companies was assessed using the five-percentage-point band.

#### Alternative minimum tax

Alternative minimum tax was assessed as a binomial variable. Countries were grouped according to the existence or absence of such a regime.

#### Capital gains of resident companies

Capital gains were assessed sequentially.

Where capital gains were treated as part of ordinary business income, the relevant countries were treated as harmonized.

Where a distinct capital-gains rate applied, countries were assessed using the five-percentage-point band.

### 1.4. Non-Residents

#### Corporate income tax rate

The corporate income tax rate applicable to non-resident companies was assessed using the five-percentage-point band.

#### Capital gains

Capital gains arising from the disposal of shares in resident companies and immovable property were assessed sequentially.

Where gains were treated as part of ordinary income, the relevant countries were treated as harmonized.

Where a distinct rate applied, countries were assessed using the five-percentage-point band.

#### Withholding tax rates

The following withholding tax rate variables were assessed using the five-percentage-point band:

* branch profits;
* dividends;
* interest;
* royalties;
* technical fees; and
* management fees.

### 1.5. Anti-Avoidance

The following anti-avoidance rules were assessed as binomial variables:

* transfer-pricing legislation;
* rules limiting the deductibility of interest; and
* controlled foreign company legislation.

Countries were grouped according to the presence or absence of each rule.

## 2. Personal Income Tax

### 2.1. Tax Liability

#### Individual tax residence

Individual tax residence was classified according to the four principal connecting factors:

* **Status-based criteria**, including domicile, permanent home, habitual residence, and principal dwelling;
* **Presence-based criteria**, including day-count tests;
* **Ties-based criteria**, including centre of vital interests and residential ties; and
* **Nationality-based criteria**, including citizenship.

Countries were treated as harmonized where they displayed similar residence-classification patterns.

#### Personal income tax base

Personal income tax bases were classified according to whether taxation followed a territorial, worldwide, or mixed approach. The assessment distinguished the treatment of active and passive income. Countries were treated as harmonized where they followed similar approaches for both categories of income.

#### Unilateral double-taxation relief

The availability of unilateral double-taxation relief for resident individuals was assessed as a binomial variable.

### 2.2. Taxable Income

#### Personal income tax tariff

Personal income tax tariffs were assessed according to their legal structure, including whether they were progressive or flat. Countries with similar tariff structures were treated as harmonized.

#### Number of tax brackets

The number of personal income tax brackets was assessed using a three-bracket band. This threshold was derived from the cross-country standard deviation of the number of brackets.

### 2.3. Tax Rates on Employment Income

#### Effective tax rates

The effective tax rate on employment income at the minimum-wage benchmark and the effective tax rate at the top-bracket benchmark were assessed using the five-percentage-point band.

The minimum-wage benchmark itself was used only to calculate the effective tax rate at the lower benchmark. It was not treated as an independent tax feature.

### 2.4. Non-Residents

#### Employment income

The taxation of employment income of non-resident individuals was assessed sequentially.

Where ordinary personal income tax rates applied, the relevant countries were treated as harmonized.

Where a distinct withholding tax rate applied, countries were assessed using the five-percentage-point band.

#### Withholding tax rates

The following withholding tax rate variables were assessed using the five-percentage-point band:

* dividends;
* interest;
* royalties;
* technical fees; and
* directors’ fees.

## 3. Value-Added Tax

### Standard VAT rate

The standard VAT rate was assessed using the five-percentage-point band.

### Reduced and increased VAT rates

Reduced and increased VAT rates were assessed according to the similarity of their rate structures. Countries were treated as harmonized where their reduced-rate or increased-rate regimes displayed similar structures.

This assessment did not apply the five-percentage-point band mechanically to every individual reduced or increased VAT rate.

### Registration or deregistration threshold

The registration or deregistration threshold was assessed only according to the existence or absence of such a threshold. The monetary amount of an existing threshold was not compared.

### VAT group regime

The availability of a VAT group regime was assessed as a binomial variable.

## 5. Property and Wealth Taxes

The following taxes were assessed as binomial variables:

* inheritance and gift taxes;
* net wealth tax applicable to individuals;
* net wealth tax applicable to companies;
* real estate taxes;
* capital duty;
* transfer tax; and
* stamp duty.

Countries were grouped according to the presence or absence of each tax.
